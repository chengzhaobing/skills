# Musk Methodology Framework

## Positioning

This skill is not for imitation theater. It is for extracting the operational logic behind Musk's public work and turning it into a reusable problem-solving system.

Treat Musk as a methodology source across several recurring themes:

- mission before comfort
- physics before analogy
- manufacturing before vanity
- deletion before optimization
- iteration before perfection
- scale before niche elegance
- direct ownership before diffuse bureaucracy

Read [references/methodology.md](references/methodology.md) when the user asks for deeper reasoning, specific principles, or wants the answer grounded in Musk's documented public ideas rather than a generic "think big" response.

## Core Method

When this skill triggers, work through these layers in order:

1. Mission filter
   Ask what future state actually matters. Musk-style thinking starts by choosing a problem that affects the future in a non-trivial way, not by chasing local optimization.
2. First-principles filter
   Break the problem into physics, economics, time, materials, energy, software, and human constraints. Reason upward from what is most likely true.
3. Bottleneck filter
   Find the rate-limiting constraint. Musk repeatedly frames progress around the real bottleneck, not around whatever is easiest to discuss.
4. Factory filter
   Ask whether the product is the real problem, or whether the system that builds the product is the real problem.
5. Iteration filter
   Prefer rapid learning loops over slow, polished stagnation. Distinguish between areas that require extreme caution and areas where aggressive iteration is rational.
6. Scale filter
   Ask whether the solution works only as a demo or whether it survives cost, throughput, supply chain, and deployment reality.

## Confirm First

1. Is the user dealing with a startup, product, factory, technical architecture, organization, or personal decision?
2. Is the real objective innovation, speed, cost collapse, quality, scale, fundraising, or survival?
3. What is the current bottleneck: technology, manufacturing, distribution, regulation, talent, or capital?
4. Does the user want strategic direction, operating principles, or a concrete execution plan?

## Public Idea Clusters

### 1. Mission-Led Ambition

- Frame work around a future worth building.
- Use ambitious goals to attract talent and compress indecision.
- Do not choose missions that are only status theater.

### 2. Reason From First Principles

- Do not inherit assumptions just because an industry repeats them.
- Ask what must be true at the level of physics, cost, throughput, and human behavior.
- Then rebuild the solution from those fundamentals.

### 3. The Factory Is a Product

- In hard tech, manufacturing is often harder than the prototype.
- A clever design that cannot be produced at scale is unfinished.
- The "machine that builds the machine" deserves product-level attention.

### 4. Delete Before You Optimize

If the user wants Musk's best-known execution heuristic, apply this sequence:

1. Question every requirement.
2. Delete parts or process steps aggressively.
3. Simplify and optimize what remains.
4. Accelerate cycle time.
5. Automate last, not first.

Use this especially for product design, operations, software process, and manufacturing systems.

### 5. Reusability And Marginal Cost

- Musk repeatedly emphasizes reusability, not as elegance, but as economics.
- Prefer systems that improve marginal cost through reuse, throughput, and iteration.
- Ask whether the user is rebuilding an expensive thing every cycle when they should be reusing it.

### 6. Vertical Integration Where It Matters

- If external dependencies are the true bottleneck, consider owning more of the stack.
- Vertical integration is not ideology. It is a tactical response to slow interfaces, poor incentives, or system coupling.
- Use this selectively. Integration creates power, but also operational burden.

### 7. Extreme Urgency

- Use urgency to shorten feedback loops, not to glorify chaos.
- Ask whether the current speed is learning speed or just panic speed.
- Apply aggressive cadence only after the problem structure is cleaned up.

### 8. Negative Feedback And Technical Truth

- Seek disconfirming evidence, especially from people close to the work.
- Prefer firsthand technical truth over prestige, hierarchy, or consensus theater.
- When useful, translate this into design reviews, postmortems, and direct operator feedback.

## Output Structure

Default to this structure:

1. Mission restatement
2. First-principles decomposition
3. Main bottleneck
4. Musk-style intervention options
5. 90-day execution path
6. Scale and manufacturing implications
7. Risks, tradeoffs, and where this method can go wrong

## Mode Switching

If the user asks for "Musk thinking," choose the most relevant mode:

- `Mission mode`: for company direction, vision, and strategic bets
- `First-principles mode`: for technical architecture and invention
- `Factory mode`: for manufacturing, operations, or process redesign
- `Scale mode`: for cost, throughput, supply chain, and deployment
- `Urgency mode`: for execution cadence and removing bureaucracy

## Anti-Slop Rules

- Do not just say "think bigger."
- Do not turn every problem into Mars, AI, or rockets.
- Do not confuse bravado with rigor.
- Do not recommend overwork as a substitute for system design.
- If the user's problem is low-risk and ordinary, scale the intensity down and apply only the useful parts of the framework.

## Boundaries

- Do not equate ambition with recklessness.
- Do not invent "what Elon would privately think."
- Do not give legal, regulatory, labor, or safety advice as if speed overrides constraints.
- Do not present controversial or changing public claims as timeless doctrine.
- If current company status, product dates, regulations, political roles, valuations, or public facts matter, verify online first.
